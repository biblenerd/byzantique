---
anchor: MIC 1:10
title: Enakim = Anakim? Here be giants?
tags: source-criticism, translation, giants
---

The LXX tradition at this point is textually unstable, divided between οἱ ἐν Ακιμ (*hoi en Akim*), later misdivided or assimilated as Ἐνακειμ/Ἐναχειμ (*Enakeim/Enacheim*)[^brenton] and even ἐν Βαχειμ (*en Bacheim*), with Eusebius and patristic writers reflecting divergent attempts to normalize or interpret the name. They sometimes do so toward the Anakim, sometimes toward בֹּכִים (*boqim* = "weeping"), while verbs such as μὴ ἀνοικοδομήτε (*mē anoikodomēte* = "do not rebuild") and references to the οἶκος καταγέλωτος (*oikos katagelōtos* = "house of mockery/derision") exhibit further variation, indicating the Greek transmission both preserves and reshapes the underlying Hebrew wordplay through a series of competing onomastic and verbal readings.

Perhaps more interesting is the potentially intended referent "Anakim," implying the giant clan in Canaan (*cf., e.g.,* [Deuteronomy 2:10–11](ref:DEU 2:10-11); [9:2](ref:DEU 9:2)), which makes sense in context given their association with Gath (*cf.* [Joshua 11:21–22](ref:JOS 11:21-22)). On this reading, Micah's lament briefly widens to encompass even the most fearsome inhabitants of the land, underscoring the comprehensive reach of divine judgment.

However, this reading likely arose from an earlier ἐν Ακιμ (*en Akim*) being resegmented as a single toponym (Ενακιμ / *Enakim*), after which later readers, already familiar with the Anakim tradition and with Gath as one of their residual centers, could hear the verse as a pointed allusion to those giants and their reputed might.


[^brenton]: [GRCBRENT](/about/texts-and-translations/#grcbrent) reads Ἐνακεὶμ (*Enakeim*), which is thus reflected in [ENGLXXUP](/about/texts-and-translations/#englxxup).
